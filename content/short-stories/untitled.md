Journalism is a business of collective showmanship. The flashy, the shiny, the novel — these made the headlines, while the relevant news that actually mattered was left to rot in the dregs of the morning e-papers. This was the pulse of the age, I mused, stirring my coffee.

How long had it been now? Thirty years plying the trade, and each day now was as weary as the rest. I had followed that man's hopeless dream for three long decades. A ghostly aspiration that had consumed the good doctor, turned him into a dilapidated carcass clutching at life. But perhaps now he would smile genuinely. After all, there she stood, towering over the cityscape on Gregor Hill, sleek and alien. The Odyssey. The "ship of dreams," they called it, capable of relativistic space flight. The science behind it was baffling to me — a hocus pocus of equations and mumbo jumbo that made me feel dizzy whenever I tried. Doctor T... ah, Untitled, as he would have preferred, had spent most of his adult life chasing what the establishment had cheerfully deemed a "hopeless dream": to build a relativistic engine, to achieve and surpass the speed of light.

Odyssey. She had been christened by her proud owners, and rightly so, for it had been that epic a struggle to birth this gargantuan thing. A thought struck me then. The last time that epithet had been used was for the beautiful Titanic, all those years past. Look how that ended. Was all human ambition a self-defeating exercise, peculiarly when applied to the mastery of the real world? I remember thinking this as I donned my rustic attire for my last battle of wits with my muse, Untitled.

Rustic. The very first time I met Untitled, then a young bright researcher at CERN, scrawny, carrying that particular air of obscurity that comes with esoteric knowledge, he had called me rustic. All these years later, standing before his small gravestone lit with an eternal flame, there is still no more apt word to describe me. Distinguished perhaps was the simplicity that bonded us. Or perhaps it was simply my faith in his wild dream. The epigraph says it all: *He who chases infinity shall become himself infinite.*

Indeed, old friend. Indeed. Such a small monument for one who now stands titan in every hall of learning. Fetes are laid in his honour where once there was only sarcasm and unbridled mocking laughter. But I digress. My old mind wanders easily. Back to the tale.

It was the winter of 21xx.

A chill had set into the breeze and my ride to Gregor Hill left me freezing. Preparation was needed for any encounter with Untitled, and my trusty cigar provided something like assurance. A pity they had banned tobacco a decade ago. Still, the artificial juice was quite the hit. Less romantic, admittedly. The fear of death and self-harm had been surgically exorcised from the pleasure, but it would suffice.

Was I sad? Honestly, my emotions then were a cocktail best described as empathic confusion. Untitled had been more than a friend. He had been a goal. Religiously, every Monday, I would show up at his offices, wait at the reception while Elaine, his secretary, completed the necessary paperwork. Quite unremarkable was this Elaine. Name and face hardly unique from any two others, yet efficient. I had often questioned Untitled as to why he kept her. This was the age of mechanization, after all. Imprints of it were everywhere, robotic butlers and maids scurrying after their fleshy masters. Efficient and routine, they had become the new face of progress. A chuckle. That was all the answer I ever received from the man. Years later it would hit me, sudden as an epiphany, that perhaps a human face simply dulled some of the loneliness inevitably felt by a man living on a hill outside society.
