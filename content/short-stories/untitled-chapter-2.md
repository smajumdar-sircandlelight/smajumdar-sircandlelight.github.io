Rustic. The very first time I met Untitled, then a young bright researcher at CERN, scrawny with that particular air of obscurity that comes with esoteric knowledge, he had called me rustic. All these years later, as I stand in front of this small gravestone lit with eternal flame, there is no more apt adjective to describe me. Perhaps it was my simplicity that bonded us, or perhaps it was my faith in his wild dream. The epigraph says it all: He who chases infinity shall become himself infinite. Indeed, old friend. Indeed. Such a small monument for one who now stands titan in every hall of learning. Fêtes are laid in his honour where once there was only sarcasm unbridled. But I digress. My old mind wanders easily. Back to the tale.

It was the winter of 21xx. A chill had set into the breeze and my ride to Gregor Hill had left me freezing. Preparation was needed to deal with Untitled, and my trusty cigar provided something like assurance. A pity they had banned tobacco a decade ago. The artificial juice was quite the hit, less romantic admittedly, now that death had been surgically removed from the pleasure, but it would suffice.

Was I sad? Honestly, my emotions then defied easy taxonomy. Untitled had been more than a friend, he had been a goal. Religiously, every Monday, I would show up at his offices and wait at the reception while Elaine, his secretary, completed the necessary paperwork. I had often questioned Untitled as to why he kept her. This was the age of mechanization, after all. Imprints of it were everywhere, robotic butlers, mechanical maids, the whole choreography of a life outsourced. His only answer was a slow smile and a blazing twinkle of the eyes that marked a private amusement. Insufferable, truly. Yet as the years progressed, it dawned on me that even Untitled, a champion of the new and the pioneering avant-garde of technology, felt the need for a human face to dull the loneliness of a man living on a hill outside society.

Untitled was slow in greeting me from his bed. He could barely move now, the years of hard living having reduced him to something gaunt and deliberate, animated chiefly by that slight twinkle in the eye, the hallmark of a man the world had since likened to genius. I asked him about Elaine, and why he had chosen to keep her on all these years.

He paused, and then said: "She has weathered me not once questioning why I am so devoted to my craft. Others have come, they have gone, they have found me rather difficult, eccentric even, but she has carried on in good faith, never complaining. I don't know if seeing her all these years has served as an anchoring point. You see, my greatest goal shifted towards the end, and in part, I think it was because of her."

"What do you mean?" I quipped. I dare say my interest was piqued. Untitled had hardly been forthcoming all these years, and while rumours had floated around his relationship with Elaine, I had always found them reductive, the world forever shrinking complexity into gossip.

He took a sip from his tumbler and replied: "Who do you think I toiled for? Was it myself? Merely my own ego that pushed me to tear down the near-sighted fools who called my life's work fantasy? Perhaps. Sometimes I wonder whether I was simply a slave to a self-fulfilling irony, and yet, in the last four years, I could grasp it. She was always tantalizingly a few months, a few more lines away. In those moments, I had a realization. Elaine. Not her, not the physical, but Her, she was human."

A coughing fit tore through him. I leaned forward, offering more of the brew placed near his bedstand. Untitled accepted the cup, sipped, and paused. His eyes caught the light strangely, that familiar twinkle but dimmed now, or perhaps deepened. Here was a man of untold knowledge, adept in sciences whose very names escaped my ability to articulate, and yet, did I sense a tinge of sorrow?
